Features of Gastrointestinal Disease Detection System

The GI disease detection system classifies endoscopic images into eight classes. Each class represents a unique condition or finding within the gastrointestinal tract. Detailed below are the classes with their descriptions and sample images to enhance understanding.

Classes and Their Features

  • Dyed-Lifted Polyps:

    Endoscopic findings where polyps are highlighted using a dye for better visibility. These are often a precursor to more detailed diagnostic procedures or surgical interventions.

  • Dyed-Resection Margins:

    Highlights the edges of resected areas to ensure complete removal of abnormal tissues. This technique helps prevent recurrence of abnormal growths.

  • Esophagitis:

    Inflammation of the esophagus, characterized by redness or irritation seen in endoscopic images. It may result from acid reflux, infections, or allergies.

  • Normal Cecum:

    Represents normal findings in the cecum, the beginning of the large intestine. Observations ensure no abnormalities such as polyps or inflammation are present.

  • Normal Pylorus:

    Depicts a normal pyloric region, which is the opening from the stomach into the duodenum. A healthy pylorus ensures proper digestion and prevents reflux.

  • Normal Z-Line:

    Shows the transition between the esophagus and stomach with no abnormalities. This junction is critical in identifying issues like Barrett's esophagus.

  • Polyps:

    Abnormal growths within the GI tract that may require further evaluation or removal. Some polyps are benign, while others can be precancerous.

  • Ulcerative Colitis:

    Chronic inflammation of the colon, often visible as ulcers or irregularities in the lining. It can lead to symptoms like abdominal pain and diarrhea.

These classifications aid in early and accurate detection of gastrointestinal anomalies, ensuring timely intervention and improved patient outcomes. Sample images provide visual cues for better understanding and diagnosis.
